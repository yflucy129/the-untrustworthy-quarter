The visible kind of mismatch

Walmart paid $45M because of a price tag.

In 2024, Walmart settled a class action alleging shelf prices and checkout prices did not match.

But there is another kind of mismatch that happens every day, and it is completely legal.

The quieter kind

USDA tracks two prices for every fruit and vegetable.

One is what you pay at the register.

The other is what you actually eat.

Data source: USDA ERS Fruit and Vegetable Prices

Shelf price $2.51 per pound bought
Real price $6.69 per edible pound

Act 2 / Magnitude

The gap runs from -14% to +167%.

Most items cluster low. A few do not.

Shelf price vs. edible price

Most produce sits on the line. Some does not.

The diagonal is honest pricing: what you buy costs the same as what you eat.

Nothing on the shelf tells you which side of the line a product is on.

Why the shelf stays quiet

For raw produce, nutrition labeling is voluntary.

FDA requires Nutrition Facts for most prepared foods. Raw produce and fish are handled differently: retailers are urged to provide point-of-purchase nutrition information, but the program is voluntary.

The shelf is not hiding the gap. No one requires it to explain the gap in the first place.

Sources: FDA food labeling overview; 21 CFR 101.42

Act 3 / The tiers

Three tiers, by how visible the waste is.

1 in 4 produce items belongs to a tier the shelf cannot help you see.

Tier labels are this project's classification of the 66 fresh USDA rows.

Forgivable

You know watermelons have rinds. You do not know they are 48%.

Average price inflation in this tier: 83%.

Deceptive

You pay for 100% of an artichoke. You eat 38%.

The shelf says $2.51. It does not say $6.69.

Hidden mechanics

Three ways the shelf hides the waste.

A rule with exceptions

Some items go the other way.

For three fresh items, USDA's yield factor is above 1, so the real-price calculation drops below the shelf price.

Act 4 / Implications

Walmart's $45M was news. This is not.

The Walmart case was about allegedly illegal pricing.

This is legal pricing.

No one, from industry to regulators to consumers, has much reason to surface it.

No useful pattern

You cannot predict deception from the price.

Cheap items can mislead you. Expensive items can be honest. There is no rule.

The workaround

Sometimes canned is cheaper. Sometimes it is not.

Even the workaround is unreliable.

Aisle comparison

The fruit aisle misleads more than the vegetable aisle.

Fruits average 39% inflation. Vegetables average 25%.

The melons, citrus, and pomegranates carry most of the gap.

Close

75% of the produce aisle tells you the truth.

The other 25% cannot.

Not because anyone is lying, but because no one has ever required the shelf to speak.
